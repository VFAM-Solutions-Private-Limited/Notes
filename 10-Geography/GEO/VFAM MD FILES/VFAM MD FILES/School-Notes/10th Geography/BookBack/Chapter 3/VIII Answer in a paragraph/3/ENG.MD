### 3. Characteristics of Intensive and Mixed Farming
 
**Intensive Farming:**
Intensive farming is a type of agriculture where the aim is to get the **maximum possible yield from a small piece of land**. Its characteristics are:
- Uses **heavy doses of chemical fertilizers** to make soil more productive
- Uses **pesticides and insecticides** to protect crops from pests and diseases
- Uses **modern machines** like tractors, harvesters, and seed drills
- Uses **High Yielding Variety (HYV) seeds** that produce more grain per plant
- The land is used **throughout the year** without giving it rest
- It results in **high productivity** but can also damage soil health over time
- Practiced mainly in states like Punjab, Haryana, and Western Uttar Pradesh
**Mixed Farming:**
Mixed farming is a system where a farmer does **many activities together** on the same farm. Its characteristics are:
- Combines **crop cultivation** with **animal rearing** (cattle, poultry, sheep)
- Also includes **fishery, bee-keeping**, and other activities
- The farmer is not dependent on just one source of income
- **Animals help crops** - they provide manure (natural fertilizer) for fields
- **Crops help animals** - crop waste (straw, leaves) is used as animal feed
- Risk of crop failure is reduced because other activities still provide income
- It is more **sustainable** and environment-friendly than intensive farming
- Practiced widely across India, especially in areas with mixed land use
