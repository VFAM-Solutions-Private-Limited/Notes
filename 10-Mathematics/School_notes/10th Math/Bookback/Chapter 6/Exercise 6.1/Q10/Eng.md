# Exercise 6.1

## Question 10:

Prove the following.

$$
\text{If }
\frac{\cos\theta}{1+\sin\theta}
=
\frac{1}{a},
\text{ then prove that }
\frac{a^2-1}{a^2+1}
=
\sin\theta.
$$

---

## Procedure:

1. Write the given condition.
2. Express \(a\) in terms of trigonometric ratios.
3. Simplify using standard identities.
4. Obtain the required result.

---

## Solution:

Given:

$$
\frac{\cos\theta}{1+\sin\theta}
=
\frac{1}{a}
$$

To prove:

$$
\frac{a^2-1}{a^2+1}
=
\sin\theta
$$

$$
a
=
\frac{1+\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}
$$

Squaring both sides,

$$
a^2
=
\frac{(1+\sin\theta)^2}{\cos^2\theta}
$$

$$
=
\frac{(1+\sin\theta)^2}
     {1-\sin^2\theta}
$$

$$
=
\frac{1+\sin\theta}
     {1-\sin\theta}
$$

Therefore,

$$
a^2-1
=
\frac{1+\sin\theta}
     {1-\sin\theta}
-1
$$

$$
=
\frac{2\sin\theta}
     {1-\sin\theta}
$$

and

$$
a^2+1
=
\frac{1+\sin\theta}
     {1-\sin\theta}
+1
$$

$$
=
\frac{2}
     {1-\sin\theta}
$$

Hence,

$$
\frac{a^2-1}{a^2+1}
=
\frac{\frac{2\sin\theta}{1-\sin\theta}}
     {\frac{2}{1-\sin\theta}}
$$

$$
=
\sin\theta
$$

$$
=LHS=RHS
$$

Hence proved.
